Same note, five treatments

One research note — the same title, the same four sections, the same AI observations — run through five different interface philosophies. All five start from the same brief: the writing is the product, and the interface's only job is to get out of its way. Switch between them below.

Quiet

Minimal macOS-native, in the spirit of Jony Ive.

Built the way Apple's own apps are built: one type family, one accent, and otherwise nothing but hairlines and air. The sidebar is barely there — translucent, borderless. AI feedback lives behind a single small dot in the corner until you ask for it; it never appears on its own.

Best forWriters who already trust their own judgment and want AI on a short leash. Trade-offRestraint can read as "where are the features" to anyone expecting more chrome.

Do Remote Teams Communicate Worse? A Structural Review

Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Patterns

Remote work hasn't eliminated communication so much as redistributed it. Teams that once relied on a five-minute hallway exchange now default to asynchronous, written coordination — Slack threads, shared docs, the occasional recorded video.

The literature is split on whether this is a net loss. Some argue that writing things down forces clearer thinking; others point to the erosion of the informal, low-stakes exchange that used to catch problems early.

The Gazette

A New Yorker editorial sensibility.

Treats every note like a galley proof: serif body copy set to a comfortable measure, a masthead instead of a toolbar, a table of contents instead of a file list. AI observations run as a copyeditor's pencil marks in the margin — a discipline borrowed whole from print, where the text is the product and everything else is production design.

Best forLong-form writing you intend to actually finish and be proud of. Trade-offThe margin column costs real width and doesn't collapse gracefully under ~1000px.

The Desk

Clean, a little playful — an Evernote-style overview.

An overview grid up front — muted tag colors, soft cards, enough white space to breathe — so you can see the shape of your whole research library before you commit to one thing. Open a note and every trace of the grid disappears: full window, nothing but the document and a thin status line. Try it — click a card.

Best forPeople juggling many notes who need to browse before they write. Trade-offTwo distinct visual modes to design and keep consistent, instead of one.

Throughline

Built to help you keep hold of your own argument.

Starts from a different question: not "how do we show AI feedback" but "how do we stop the writer from losing their argument." The research question stays pinned at the top at all times. A quiet spine down the left turns your outline into a structure map — thin ring where a section is underdeveloped. AI doesn't file a report; it changes the color of one ambient dot.

The Archive

A card catalog for a researcher's own thinking.

Reframes note-taking as a card catalog: each note is an index card with a stamped date and a call number, and related notes surface as a few cards pulled loose from the stack instead of a search box. It leans on association over hierarchy, closer to a Zettelkasten than a file system — on the theory that a researcher thinks better when they can see what else they've already written that's relevant. Click a card.

Best forCumulative research over months, where the value is in connections you'd forget you made. Trade-offNeeds a real cross-note linking model — the payoff shows up at note #50, not note #5.
